## Sector Call — Too Much Capacity, Not Enough Patients

We downgrade the Indian hospital sector to UNDERWEIGHT, driven by our analysis showing a significant upcoming supply-demand mismatch in key urban clusters. The top 6 listed hospital chains are collectively adding ~15,000 beds over FY26-FY28 — a 25% increase in organized capacity — against our estimate of 12-15% demand growth in the same period. This will inevitably pressure occupancy rates and ARPOB across the sector.

Max Healthcare is our top SELL in the sector given (a) its premium valuation at 26x forward EV/EBITDA, (b) concentrated NCR exposure where capacity additions are most acute, and (c) the market's overly optimistic margin assumptions that ignore greenfield dilution.

## Max Healthcare — Specific Concerns

### 1. NCR Capacity Glut
Max Healthcare derives ~65% of revenue from the Delhi-NCR market. Our supply-side mapping shows:
- **Max Healthcare**: +900 beds (Saket 400 + Gurgaon 500) by FY27
- **Fortis Healthcare**: +350 beds (Gurugram expansion) by Q1 FY27
- **Medanta**: +500 beds (Noida greenfield) by mid-FY27
- **Smaller chains + standalone**: ~400 beds across NCR

Total: ~2,150 net new beds in NCR alone, against an existing base of ~12,000 organized beds. This represents an 18% capacity increase in a market where we estimate demand growth of only 8-10%.

### 2. ARPOB Under Structural Pressure
ARPOB for Max has been flat at ₹77-78K for three quarters despite management's stated focus on complex case mix. We believe this reflects:
- **Insurance company resistance**: Major insurers (Star Health, ICICI Lombard) are increasingly pushing back on procedure pricing through pre-authorization tightening
- **Government scheme penetration**: Ayushman Bharat and state-level schemes are growing as a share of the payor mix, carrying 40-60% discounts to rack rates
- **Price transparency**: Online comparison platforms are enabling patients to shop across hospitals, compressing pricing premiums for branded chains

### 3. Margin Expectations Are Too High
Consensus models 26.2% EBITDA margin for FY27. We model 23.5% based on:
- Pre-operative costs for 5 simultaneously commissioning sites (~150 bps drag)
- Ramp-up losses at Sector 56 Gurgaon greenfield — we model ₹40-50 Cr of losses in the first 18 months
- Staff hiring ahead of commissioning (Max has indicated plans to hire 2,000+ staff across new facilities)
- Energy and consumables inflation of 5-7% that cannot be fully passed through to insurers

### 4. Occupancy Overshoot Is Temporary
The Q2 FY26 occupancy of 77% was impressive but we caution against extrapolation:
- Q2 benefits from elective surgery seasonality (monsoon-related respiratory admissions, pre-Diwali surgery scheduling)
- Q3-Q4 typically shows 200-300 bps of seasonal decline in occupancy
- As new capacity comes online, blended occupancy will mechanically decline as the denominator expands faster than the numerator

## Where We Could Be Wrong

- **Demand surprise**: If India's healthcare utilization rates increase faster than historical trends (driven by rising health insurance penetration, post-COVID health awareness), the capacity additions could be absorbed without occupancy dilution
- **M&A**: If Max deploys its ₹2,000 Cr cash pile for accretive acquisitions at <12x EV/EBITDA, it could offset the greenfield dilution
- **International medical tourism**: A meaningful acceleration in medical tourism volumes (currently <5% of revenue) could provide occupancy support independent of domestic demand trends
- **Operating leverage surprise**: If Max's brownfield expansions achieve >80% occupancy within 12 months (vs our 18-month assumption), margin dilution would be significantly lower
